3.1 Body weight

Respondents were asked, “Right now, do you feel you are…” and were given the following options:

  • Underweight
  • Slightly underweight
  • Just about right
  • Slightly overweight
  • Overweight

It is important to note that this is a perception of current bodyweight and not a response that should be expected to correlate closely to actual body mass index definitions of underweight, normal weight, and overweight.

The following figure shows the distribution of responses for males and females, separately.

(#fig:body_weight-fig)Respondent’s self-reported body weight by gender

Males were much less likely to respond with “Overweight” and more likely to respond with “Just about right” and both underweight categories. The chi-square test comparing the distribution of responses by gender is very statistically significant, indicating a different distribution of self-reported current bodyweight for males and females.

Respondents were also asked, “Have you tried to lose any weight in the past 12 months?” with option of either Yes or No. A little over half of all respondents reported trying to lose weight in the past 12 months.

The following figure compares respondent’s self-reported current bodyweight with responses for trying to lose weight in the past year.

(#fig:body_weight_lose-fig)Respondent’s self-reported body weight by trying to lose weight

Those that reported trying to lose weight in the past year were much more likely to report being “Slightly overweight” or “Overweight” compared to those reporting they did not try to lose weight.

3.2 Body type

Respondents were given a figure to select a current body type and ideal body type for themselves. The following image provides this figure along with the number assigned to each image.

The Figure Rating Scale (Stunkard’s Scale)

Higher numbers tend to correlate with a more overweight body type and lower numbers to a more underweight body type.

3.2.1 Current body type

Next, respondents were asked to identify in the above figure, their current body type.

(#fig:body_current-fig)Respondent’s self-reported current body type by gender

Males are most likely to report a body type of 6 while females a body type of 5. Overall, there is a statistically significant difference in the distribution of current body types reported by males and females.

We expect respondents that indicate a larger current body type to be much more likely to indicating trying to lose weight in the past year. The following figure shows this trend with nearly all individuals with a current body type of 1 responding with “No” and nearly all individuals with a current body type of 9 responding with “Yes”. Further, there is a clear trend from the more underweight current body type to the more overweight body type in the proportion of individuals trying to lose weight in the past year.

(#fig:body_current_lose-fig)Respondent’s self-reported current body type by trying to lose weight

We also expect a strong correlation between current body type and self-reported current bodyweight.

(#fig:body_current_weight-fig)Correlation of respondent’s self-reported current body type and bodyweight

There is a strong correlation between current bodyweight and body type. Respondents indicating they are “Overweight” tend to provide a current body type between 6 and 8. Respodnents indicating they are slightly overweight are most likely to provide a current body type of 5 and typically between 4 and 6. Respondents indicating that their current bodyweight is “Just about right” are most likely to provide a current body type of 4.

3.2.2 Current body type

Finally, respondents were asked to identify in the above figure, their ideal body type.

(#fig:body_ideal-fig)Respondent’s self-reported ideal body type by gender

Males and females provide a statistically significant different distribution of ideal body types. Males are most likely to provide an ideal body type of 5 while females an ideal body type of 4. Further, females were much more likely to identify 2 and 3 as ideal body types when compared to males.

We expect there to be some correlation between ideal and current body types as shown in the following figure.

(#fig:body_current_ideal-fig)Correlation of respondent’s self-reported current body type and bodyweight

Respondents tend to provide an ideal body type that is 1 to 2 levels lower than respondents’ current body type. For example, those with a current body type of 5 were most likely to provide an ideal body type of 4 followed by 3 and 5. Those with a current body type of 6 were most likely to provide an ideal body type of 4 followed by 5. Those with a current body type of 4 were most likely to provide an ideal body type of 3. The generally positive correlation (direction of lighter color) in the figure indicates that as current body type increases, ideal body type also increases, possibly suggesting that ideal body type is highly influenced by current body type (and therefore current bodyweight) rather than being an abstract concept outside of an individual’s current condition.
